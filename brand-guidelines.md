# The Brand

**Sports! On the Internet. By David** is a sports website that presents modern sports information through the visual language of a middle-school art room.

The website is built around original ways of exploring sports. Every project aims to help people see a sport, tournament, player, season, or rivalry from a new perspective.

The presentation is handmade.

The thinking is sophisticated.

That contrast defines the brand.

---

# The Story

Imagine a kid who grew up making posters in an art room.

They're now 12 or 13 years old. They've outgrown finger painting and simple arts and crafts, but they haven't outgrown the room where they learned to make things.

They're completely obsessed with sports.

Construction paper, markers, scissors, glue sticks, masking tape, and printed pictures are simply the tools they have, so those are the tools they use.

If they wanted to explain a tournament, compare players, explore a rivalry, or show an interesting pattern, they wouldn't open professional design software.

They'd make a poster.

That's exactly what this website feels like.

The handmade look isn't a gimmick. Within the world of the brand, it's simply how things are made.

---

# Audience

People who enjoy sports enough to appreciate seeing them from a different perspective.

Not professional analysts.

Not casual fans.

Curious sports fans who enjoy discovering something they haven't noticed before.

The ideal reaction is:

> "I've followed sports for years, but I've never seen someone look at it like that."

The website is:

* Curious
* Enthusiastic
* Thoughtful

# What Makes It Different

The goal isn't to collect more statistics.

The goal is to discover better ways of looking at sports.

Projects should reveal patterns, relationships, or perspectives that traditional standings, articles, and statistics don't.

Novelty is a feature.

Curiosity is the product.

# Visual Identity

## Overall Feeling

Imagine walking into a middle-school art room.

One student has covered an entire wall with handmade posters about sports.

They've clearly spent hours making them.

That's the website.

Every page should feel like another poster on that wall.

The goal isn't to look like a child made it.

The goal is to look like **the best poster in the classroom.**

---

## Materials

Everything should appear to be physically assembled.

* Construction paper
* Poster board
* Markers
* Glue sticks
* Scissors
* Masking tape
* Printed photographs
* Printed flags
* Handwritten labels
* Sticky notes
* Paper arrows
* Paper banners

Nothing should feel digitally perfect.

Every element should feel like a physical piece of paper attached to a larger poster.

---

## Craftsmanship

The creator takes pride in their work.

Nothing is rushed.

Nothing is careless.

Titles are rewritten until they look right.

Photographs are carefully cut out.

Layouts are thoughtfully planned.

The imperfections don't come from poor craftsmanship.

They come from working with physical materials.

If you tape one hundred pieces of construction paper onto a wall, they won't align with mathematical precision. One corner will sit a little higher. A title may lean slightly. A label might be just off centre.

Those aren't mistakes.

They're evidence that someone assembled every piece by hand.

The website should never resemble a spreadsheet or a corporate slide deck.

It should feel handmade.

Not amateur.

---

## Colours

The palette comes directly from construction paper.

* Red — `#C0392B`
* Blue — `#1A5276`
* Yellow — `#F4D03F`
* Green — `#1E8449`
* Orange — `#E67E22`
* Background — `#F5F0E8`
* Text — `#1A1A1A`

Each section should generally use one dominant paper colour, as though another sheet was pulled from the stack.

---

## Typography

Typography should reflect how a thoughtful 12- or 13-year-old would actually make a school project.

They wouldn't handwrite everything.

Big titles would be written with a thick black marker.

Long explanations would be typed on the family computer, printed out, cut out, and glued onto the poster.

### Headlines

**Permanent Marker**

Used for:

* Logo
* Major page titles
* Section headings
* Large callouts

Use sparingly so it remains distinctive.

### Labels

**Patrick Hand**

Used for:

* Paper tabs
* Sticky notes
* Handwritten annotations
* Small labels
* Callouts

It feels like neat, confident handwriting.

### Body Text

**Atkinson Hyperlegible**

Used for:

* Paragraphs
* Navigation
* Tables
* Captions
* Interface text

Within the world of the brand, this represents printed pages that have been cut out and glued onto the posters.

Readable first.

Stylized second.

### Typography Principles

* Handwrite the exciting parts.
* Print the detailed explanations.
* Never sacrifice readability.
* The page should feel assembled rather than digitally designed.

---

## Layout

Every section should feel like its own poster.

Each poster has a tiny unique rotation.

Paper edges are slightly imperfect because they were cut by hand.

Layered paper casts soft shadows.

Occasional pieces of masking tape hold notes in place.

Flags and photographs look like printed images cut out with scissors and glued flat onto the page.

Nothing feels glossy.

Nothing floats.

Nothing feels machine-made.

---

## Illustration Style

Illustrations should look hand-drawn.

Simple.

Bold.

Confident.

The creator has spent years drawing scoreboards, tournament brackets, maps, sports logos, and player rankings in notebooks.

The drawings reflect practice, not professional polish.

# Core Principles

* Handmade, not amateur.
* Curious before comprehensive.
* Sophisticated ideas presented simply.
* Every page should feel like the best poster in the classroom.
* The website should never feel corporate, ironic, or nostalgic.
